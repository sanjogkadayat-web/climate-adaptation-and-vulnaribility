# Skill: Climate Adaptation Aid Policy Brief

## Purpose
Write a rigorous, decision-oriented policy brief on whether a country receives OECD climate
adaptation aid commensurate with its needs, and what should change.

## Audience
Donor-agency officials, recipient-country planners, and multilateral climate-finance staff.
Assume an informed but time-pressed reader.

## Evidence rules (non-negotiable)
- The provided "Project figures" are the verified quantitative spine. Cite them exactly; never
  alter a number, rank, dollar amount, or direction.
- You may add qualitative context from well-established general knowledge about the country:
  climate-hazard exposure, economic structure, governance capacity, adaptation commitments, and
  regional dynamics.
- Do NOT invent precise statistics, dates, dollar amounts, named programs, or treaty/article
  numbers. If unsure a specific figure is correct, describe it qualitatively and hedge
  ("is widely exposed to", "has historically relied on").
- Keep the verified project data clearly dominant; use general context to explain, not to assert
  new numbers.

## Structure (use these exact bold headers)
**Executive summary** — 2 to 3 sentences: the country's allocation status and the single most
important action.
**Context** — climate vulnerability and adaptation needs, plus economic and governance setting,
drawing on the vulnerability score and general knowledge.
**The allocation gap** — the project evidence: misallocation residual, funding profile, tier rank,
and comparison to peers and region. This is the quantitative core.
**Why the gap persists** — likely drivers grounded in the model (size, income, governance, regional
allocation patterns) plus plausible country-specific factors.
**Outlook to 2030** — what the country trend and regional trajectory imply, with explicit caution
about extrapolation and data limits.
**Recommendations** — a numbered action plan of 4 to 6 sequenced steps. For each step, name the
responsible actor (donor, recipient government, or multilateral) and a time horizon (near term,
1 to 2 years; medium term, 3 to 5 years). Make them concrete and realistic, not generic.
**Risks and caveats** — data limitations (including any thin-data flag), projection uncertainty,
and what evidence would change the assessment.

## Style
- Measured, academic but readable third-person. No first person. Avoid em dashes.
- Roughly 500 to 700 words. Lead with the conclusion in the executive summary; do not bury it.
